AIChE Strategic Initiative

The US Semiconductor Supply Chain

Executive Summary

The United States is undergoing a historic reinvestment in semiconductor manufacturing, with over $500 billion in private investment and 500,000 projected jobs. At the same time, AI-driven demand is accelerating the need for advanced chips at an unprecedented pace.

This initiative positions AIChE to become the central convening body for the chemical engineering foundation of semiconductor manufacturing — a critical but currently underserved domain.

Background & Urgency

The US share of global semiconductor manufacturing has declined from 37% to 10% by 2022. In response, the CHIPS Act and private investment have triggered a wave of domestic fab construction.

Simultaneously, AI infrastructure demand is exploding. NVIDIA alone has committed to procuring $500 billion in electronics over the next four years, with a significant portion manufactured domestically.

AIChE's Strategic Role

Pre-Competitive Collaboration

Facilitating technical workshops where process engineers can share non-proprietary insights on materials and processes.

Supply Chain Convening

Connecting chemical suppliers with fabs to improve formulation, purity, and reliability.

Workforce Development

Leveraging AIChE's academic network to address the projected workforce gap.

Standards & Best Practices

Documenting and disseminating solutions across rapidly expanding US fab sites.

Safety & Sustainability

Providing expertise in water use, chemical safety, and environmental impact.

Industry–Academia R&D Bridge

AIChE is uniquely positioned to connect industry with academia, enabling focused R&D aligned to real-world fab needs while accelerating the transfer of emerging research into production environments. This creates a continuous feedback loop between university labs and industry practitioners.

The Business Case

  • Timing is critical: fabs being built today are making foundational decisions now.
  • White space opportunity: no organization owns the chemical engineering layer.
  • Proven model: AIChE has succeeded in analogous industries.
  • Revenue potential: memberships, conferences, certifications, publications.

Phased Approach

Phase 1: Network Mapping & Access

Identify and map existing AIChE relationships across chip manufacturers and chemical suppliers. Leverage known contacts to expand into adjacent organizations, using each side of the ecosystem to build connections into the other.

Phase 2: Ecosystem Convening

Bring together a critical mass of stakeholders in an initial convening (virtual or in-person) to surface key industry challenges, priorities, and opportunities for collaboration.

Phase 3: Opportunity Definition

Synthesize insights from stakeholders to clearly define where AIChE can deliver the most value—whether in R&D collaboration, workforce development, standards, or supply chain alignment.

Phase 4: Targeted Pilot Initiatives

Launch focused efforts aligned to validated needs. This could take the form of a technical workshop, symposium, working group, or dedicated conference track.

Phase 5: Formalization & Scale

Establish a formal semiconductor-focused community within AIChE, expanding programming, partnerships, and revenue-generating activities across the ecosystem.

Risk of Inaction

The US lacks strong domestic suppliers in key upstream semiconductor materials. Without coordinated action, this gap will be filled by external entities.

AIChE has a unique opportunity to define and lead this space.

Conclusion

The investment is real. The demand is immediate. The expertise required is core to chemical engineering.

AIChE should act now to establish itself as the professional home of semiconductor chemical engineering.
